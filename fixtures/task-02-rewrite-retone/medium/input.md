So I wanted to put down some thoughts on where we landed with the warehouse pilot, because a few people keep asking me in the hallway and I'd rather just write it once. The short version is that it mostly worked, but not in the way we expected going in, and there are a couple of things we got plain wrong that we should own before the steering committee asks about them.

Let me back up. The whole point of the pilot was to see whether shifting the Eastbrook facility to a wave-based picking model would cut the time orders sit in staging before they go out the door. We'd been hearing for months that staging was the bottleneck, and the gut feeling on the floor was that pickers were running all over the place chasing single items instead of batching trips. So we ran it for six weeks, January into mid-February, with the night shift as the test group and the day shift left alone as a control.

Here's the thing nobody wants to say out loud: the staging delay was never really the problem. When we actually pulled the numbers, orders were sitting in staging for an average of forty minutes, which sounds bad until you realize the trucks weren't showing up any faster than that anyway. We were optimizing a queue that fed into another queue. The carrier pickup windows are the real constraint, and no amount of clever picking fixes a truck that arrives at two when the order was ready at noon. That's a humbling thing to discover five weeks into a six-week pilot.

That said, the wave model did do something useful, just not the thing we sold it on. Picker walking distance dropped a lot — we clocked it at roughly thirty percent less ground covered per shift, because batching the trips meant fewer return runs to the same aisle. The team noticed too. A couple of the veteran pickers, the ones who've been here longer than I have and are usually the first to roll their eyes at a new system, actually came up and said the night went smoother. That kind of buy-in is worth something, and I don't want it to get lost in a report that focuses only on the staging miss.

Where it got messy was the handheld scanners. We assumed the existing units could handle the wave software and they could not, or at least not reliably. About one in six scans either timed out or dropped the batch assignment, which meant a picker would walk to an aisle, find the screen had forgotten what it sent them for, and have to radio the supervisor to reassign. That happened enough that it probably ate most of the walking-distance gains in raw minutes, even if it didn't show up cleanly in the data. If we do this for real we need new hardware, full stop, and that's a capital line nobody budgeted for.

There's also a smaller wrinkle on the data collection itself that I should mention, because it shaped how confident I am in these numbers. We were logging walking distance off the scanner pings, triangulated from the floor beacons, and that's a decent proxy but it's not gospel. When a scanner dropped its batch and went quiet for a few minutes, those gaps just don't show up as distance at all, so it's plausible we're actually understating how much walking the broken units caused. I'd treat the thirty percent figure as directionally right but soft, and I wouldn't put it in a headline slide without that caveat attached.

On the people side, there's a thing I want to flag carefully. The wave model concentrates the picking into tighter time blocks, which is efficient, but it also means there are stretches of the shift where there's genuinely not much to do, and then a crush. A few folks said they preferred the steadier pace of the old way even if it meant more walking, because the new rhythm felt like feast or famine. I don't think that kills the idea, but if we roll it out wider we should think about how the shift is structured around it, not just drop the software on top of the existing schedule and call it done.

The other group I haven't mentioned yet is the supervisors, and they had a rougher pilot than anyone. Every dropped batch turned into a radio call they had to field, on top of their normal duties, and by week three two of them were openly frustrated. I don't blame them. We essentially asked them to be the manual fallback for software that wasn't ready, and we didn't staff for it or warn them it'd be part of the job. If there's one apology I'd make about how we ran this, it's that we leaned on the supervisors to paper over a hardware gap and never said thank you in any real way. That's worth fixing before we ask them to do another pilot.

Cost-wise, the pilot itself came in under what we set aside, mostly because we didn't have to pay for the scanner upgrade — we just limped along with the old ones and absorbed the reliability hit. So I'd caution against reading the pilot budget as any kind of guide to what a full rollout costs. The real number is the hardware refresh plus probably some consulting time to redo the shift planning, and I'd want a proper estimate before we commit to anything in front of the committee.

If you ask me what we should do next, I'd say don't expand the picking model yet. Instead, go after the carrier windows, because that's where the actual hours are hiding. I had a preliminary conversation with the logistics group and they think there's room to renegotiate two of the three pickup slots, which on its own might do more for our throughput than the whole picking change. We can keep the wave model in our back pocket for Eastbrook specifically, where the layout happens to suit it, but I wouldn't generalize it to the other two sites until we've seen it work with hardware that actually keeps up.

One last thing, and this is more of a process note. We almost didn't catch the staging-versus-carrier issue because we were so focused on the metric we'd decided to measure before the pilot started. We set out to measure staging time and we measured it beautifully, and we nearly missed that we were measuring the wrong thing entirely. I'd like us to build a checkpoint into the next pilot, maybe at the halfway mark, where we deliberately ask whether the thing we're measuring is still the thing that matters. It cost us almost nothing this time because the pilot was cheap, but on a bigger bet that kind of tunnel vision could get expensive fast.

Anyway, that's the honest picture. Happy to walk anyone through the raw data if they want it, and I'll have a tidier one-pager ready before the committee meets next week.
