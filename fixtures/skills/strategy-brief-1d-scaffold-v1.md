# Format Example — Strategy Brief Style Guide

Write a complete strategy brief using the structure shown below. The example is from an unrelated brief (a tutoring nonprofit deciding whether to open a second location) and shows the required format only — do not reuse its content.

## Situation
Northside Tutoring Collective runs at capacity, delivering 300 student sessions a week from one site with a growing waitlist. A partner school has offered free classroom space across town for 18 months, and the board must accept or decline by March.

## Objectives
Serve more students without running a cash deficit; keep the volunteer tutor base engaged; reach a decision inside the offer window.

## Options Considered
Accept the classroom space as a second site. Upside: roughly doubles session capacity in the neighborhood with the longest waitlist. Risk: splits tutors and coordinators across two locations and adds insurance and materials costs the budget has not absorbed.

Add evening hours at the current site. Upside: more sessions with no new space to manage. Risk: gains are capped by room count, and evenings are already the hardest slot to staff.

Decline and build an online tutoring program. Upside: reaches students who cannot travel to either site. Risk: platform and training costs are ongoing, and online sessions drew weaker attendance in past pilots.

## Recommendation
Accept the classroom space as an 18-month pilot limited to weekday afternoons. It is the only option that adds real capacity inside the decision window, and the time-boxed scope keeps added costs within current reserves while demand is proven.

## Risks & Mitigations
Tutor coverage thins across two sites — recruit a dedicated cohort near the partner school and stagger existing schedules. New-site demand disappoints — set a six-month session-count checkpoint with a pre-agreed wind-down plan. Coordination overhead grows — name a single site lead with a weekly cross-site check-in.

## Next Steps
Weeks 1–2: program lead confirms the space meets insurance and safety requirements. Weeks 3–6: director signs the space agreement and orders furniture and materials. Weeks 7–10: volunteer coordinator recruits and trains the new tutor cohort. Weeks 11–13: soft-open weekday afternoons and report session counts to the board.

Rules: write all six sections, in this order — Situation (two lines: the current state and why a decision is needed now), Objectives (the two or three outcomes the strategy must achieve), Options Considered (each realistic option analyzed in depth: its upside, its risk, and the trade-offs between them, even-handed), Recommendation (the single course of action, stated plainly, with the full reasoning that makes it the best fit), Risks & Mitigations (each key risk paired with a concrete mitigation), Next Steps (a sequenced plan with owners and rough timing for the first 90 days). The example above shows the format only — your brief should go substantially deeper in every section: the complete brief should run about 1,400 words (within ±10%). Ground every claim in the provided context; elaborate and reason from what is given, but do not invent facts or figures.
